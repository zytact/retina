End-to-end architecture from OCTA scans to diagnosis

An explainer at the right altitude: technical enough to defend the modeling choices, but simple enough to describe as one coherent pipeline. The system covers data curation, layer-specific representation learning, multimodal fusion, prototype-augmented classification with uncertainty, calibration, explainability, and a full evaluation suite.

Core idea

The system treats each retinal OCTA layer as a different physiological view, learns a separate visual representation for each one, models cross-layer interactions via attention, fuses those learned features with clinical metadata and generated vascular biomarkers, then produces a calibrated, uncertainty-aware disease prediction with full explainability.

Inputs Three OCTA layers, per-eye clinical metadata, generated pseudo-masks and biomarkers
Fusion Cross-layer attention, multimodal fusion, mask encoder, prototype-augmented classifier
Outputs Disease probabilities, MC-Dropout uncertainty, ECE-calibrated confidence, Grad-CAM and SHAP explanations

How to explain it

A concise guide for talking through the architecture with a supervisor or collaborator. Each row states the design choice, then the scientific reason it exists.

Why three image streams

The superficial vascular complex, deep vascular complex, and choriocapillaris are not interchangeable pictures of the same thing. They reflect different anatomical compartments and different microvascular failure modes.

Because of that, the model does not force one shared encoder across all layers. Each stream learns its own texture vocabulary before any fusion happens.

Why self-supervised pretraining

Natural-image pretraining is a weak starting point for grayscale OCTA. SimCLR is used to adapt the backbones to retinal vascular structure before supervised disease classification begins.

The bilateral OD/OS pairing is especially useful because both eyes of the same patient share systemic disease context while still providing non-identical anatomy.

Why tabular fusion matters

Image findings are not clinically interpreted in isolation. The same DVC rarefaction pattern means something different in a diabetic patient than in a non-diabetic patient.

The tabular branch therefore acts as context, not as an afterthought. Clinical features and OCTA-derived biomarkers are encoded, then used to condition the visual decision state.

Why generated masks are included

The dataset does not provide manual vessel or FAZ annotations, so the pipeline generates deterministic pseudo-masks and extracts vascular biomarkers from them.

That gives the classifier two complementary signals: dense deep features from the raw images, and more structured morphology-oriented evidence from the pseudo-mask branch.

Why attention appears twice

The first attention block is intra-image: it models how the three retinal layers interact. The second attention block is inter-modality: it lets the fused OCTA state query clinical context selectively.

This separation keeps the biological question and the multimodal question distinct, which makes the architecture easier to justify and easier to interpret.

Why prototype augmentation

The RASTA cohorts are imbalanced. A standard linear classifier tends to bias toward majority classes. A prototype-based similarity branch provides a non-parametric signal that works well for rare classes — a sample close to its class prototype scores high regardless of class frequency.

EMA class prototypes are maintained during training and combined with the linear logits via a learned scalar weight. This hybrid head improves generalisation for minority cohorts without requiring oversampling.

Why the 3-stage training schedule

Training everything from scratch would destroy the SimCLR-adapted features before they can help. Stage 1 completes self-supervised pretraining. Stage 2 trains all downstream heads with OCTA backbones frozen. Stage 3 fine-tunes end-to-end with a 10x lower backbone learning rate.

This schedule preserves layer-specific visual representations while allowing the classifier, attention, and fusion modules to learn their specialised functions before the backbones are adjusted.

Why evaluation is comprehensive

Clinical ML requires evidence beyond a single test-set accuracy. The pipeline includes 15 ablation configurations, 8 baseline comparisons, 5-fold patient-grouped cross-validation with bootstrap confidence intervals, fairness analysis across demographic subgroups, and formal statistical tests.

This is not just for publication rigour. It helps collaborators understand which modality combinations drive performance and whether the model behaves equitably across patient populations.

Why calibration, uncertainty, and explainability are explicit stages

For a clinical screening model, probability quality matters, not just ranking accuracy. The system includes post-training temperature scaling, MC-Dropout uncertainty with entropy-based flagging, and ECE/Brier metrics.

Layer dominance, Grad-CAM maps, SHAP attribution, and ablation studies provide evidence about whether the model uses plausible retinal signals rather than shortcut correlations. These are implemented as first-class outputs, not afterthoughts.

  • 3-stage training strategy (SimCLR reuse, frozen backbones, end-to-end fine-tune), 5-fold CV.
  • Explainability: Grad-CAM per layer, layer-dominance analysis, SHAP/occlusion attribution.

  • Data augmentation: synchronised OCTA transforms, mask-aware, clinical noise.
