Phase 1 — Foundation

Know Your Data

Before we build any models, we must thoroughly understand the dataset we are working with. Phase 1 is the systematic audit of the RASTA dataset: who is in it, what measurements exist, what is missing, and what traps could invalidate our conclusions later.

Why This Matters

Every modeling mistake we can catch here saves weeks of wasted training later. Class imbalance, missing data patterns, confounders, and data leakage are the most common reasons machine learning papers fail to reproduce.

Why Do We Need a Dataset Analysis Phase?

Imagine a doctor who has never seen your patient before, and you hand them a chart with numbers scrawled in the margins and a few pages torn out. That is what it feels like to train a deep learning model on a dataset you have not audited. Phase 1 is the step where we open every folder, check every file, and build a complete picture of the data landscape before we write a single line of training code.

More concretely, Phase 1 answers questions like: How many patients do we actually have? Are all four cohorts roughly equally represented, or is one group tiny? Are both eyes available for every patient? Which clinical measurements were recorded for everyone, and which are patchy? Could age differences between cohorts masquerade as disease signals? Could the same patient appear in more than one cohort by accident? These are not hypotheticals -- they routinely derail medical AI studies.

Beginners: Think of Phase 1 as the "look before you leap" step. It is not glamorous, but it is the single highest-ROI activity in the entire project. Every plot you generate here will be cited in the paper's "Dataset" section.

What Exactly Do We Check? (Research Protocol)

The analysis breaks down into several distinct investigations, each targeting a specific risk to model validity:

1. Sample Inventory

We scan the RASTA directory tree and count everything: total patients, total eyes (remember: one patient can contribute one or two eyes), eyes per cohort, and the ratio of unilateral (one eye) to bilateral (both eyes) patients. This gives us the raw N for every analysis we will later report.

2. Class Distribution and Imbalance

Disease cohorts in medical datasets are rarely equal. One group might be twice the size of another. We compute the exact count per cohort and the imbalance ratio (largest / smallest). Why does this matter? Because if we train a classifier on a 90/10 split without adjustment, it can achieve 90% accuracy by guessing the majority class every time -- yet be completely useless clinically. The imbalance ratio informs our choice of class weights, sampling strategy, and evaluation metrics later in Phase 10 and Phase 14.

3. Bilateral Eye Availability

Some patients have both eyes scanned (OD = right, OS = left); others have only one. This matters for two reasons. First, two eyes from the same patient are not independent -- they share genetics, systemic health, and lifestyle. If we treat them as independent samples, we artificially inflate our effective sample size. Second, our SimCLR pretraining strategy in Phase 3 uses OD/OS pairs as natural positive pairs. Knowing who has both eyes tells us how many of those pairs we can construct.

4. Clinical Feature Missingness

Clinical data is almost never complete. Age might be recorded for everyone; BMI might be missing for 20% of patients; smoking history might be inconsistently collected across cohorts. We build a missingness heatmap -- a grid showing which values are present (green) or absent (red) for each patient-feature combination. We also compute missingness per cohort, because systematic missingness (e.g., "cohort A never recorded BMI") can introduce bias that a model might exploit spuriously.

5. Demographic Distributions

For each cohort, we plot age, sex, and BMI distributions. If one cohort is significantly older on average, any model that relies on age-related vascular changes might appear to distinguish cohorts when it is really just detecting age. This is called a confound. We flag these for later mitigation.

6. Image Resolution and Quality

Not every OCTA scan is usable. Some may have different resolutions, watermark artifacts, motion blur, or low signal-to-noise ratio. We audit image dimensions, bit depth, and run a lightweight quality check to flag scans that should be excluded before training. This prevents garbage-in-garbage-out.

7. Layer Availability

Our architecture uses three retinal layers: Superficial Vascular Complex (SVC), Deep Vascular Complex (DVC), and Choriocapillaris (CC). We verify that all three layers exist for every eye. If one layer is missing for some patients, we need a strategy (imputation, exclusion, or a missing-layer token).

8. Confounder Analysis

We explicitly test for associations between cohort membership and demographics. Age-disease correlation, sex imbalance, and BMI differences are computed and reported. Any significant association is a confound that must be discussed in the paper and potentially adjusted for in the model.

9. Data Leakage Risk Assessment

The most embarrassing error in medical AI: accidentally having the same patient in both training and test sets. We verify that no patient ID appears in more than one cohort directory and, later in Phase 2, that all eyes from a given patient end up in the same cross-validation fold. A single leaked patient can inflate AUC by 0.1 or more.

10. Biomarker Boxplots

The clinical spreadsheet contains quantitative OCTA biomarkers (vessel densities, perfusion densities, FAZ areas) from the commercial OCTA device software. We generate per-cohort boxplots for these biomarkers. They give us an early signal: if cohorts already separate on simple biomarker measurements, that tells us the signal is present in the data. If they completely overlap, the classification problem is harder.

Important: Phase 1 biomarkers come from the commercial device software, not from our later generated masks (Phase 8). They serve as a reference baseline. We keep them separate in all analyses.

How This Fits in a Research Paper

In a publication, Phase 1 findings appear in the "Dataset" subsection of "Materials and Methods." You would present a Table 1 with demographic summaries per cohort (age mean/SD, sex count, BMI mean/SD), a Supplementary Figure 1 showing the missingness heatmap, and a Supplementary Table 1 with class counts and imbalance ratios. The confounder analysis appears in the "Statistical Analysis" section or as a supplementary note.

Crucially, by doing Phase 1 thoroughly and reporting it transparently, you demonstrate that you understand your data's limitations -- a sign of methodological rigor that reviewers value highly.

Glossary for Beginners

OD / OS
Medical abbreviations for right eye (OD = oculus dexter) and left eye (OS = oculus sinister).
Cohort
A group of patients sharing a common characteristic. Here, the four cohorts are defined by disease/risk status: RETINORM (control), ORNET (sleep apnea), FAMILIPO (familial hypercholesterolemia), MRCC (cardiac surgery / CAD).
Class Imbalance
When one category (class) has many more samples than another. Common in medical datasets because some diseases are rarer than others.
Confound
A variable that is correlated with both the input features and the target label, creating a spurious association. For example, if older patients are more likely to have CAD, the model might learn "age" instead of learning retinal patterns of disease.
Data Leakage
When information from outside the training set influences the model, typically because the same patient appears in both training and test sets. This makes performance look artificially good.
Missingness
The pattern of absent values in a dataset. Some values are missing completely at random (MCAR), some are missing for systematic reasons (e.g., a specific clinic did not record BMI).
