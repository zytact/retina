Preprocessing explainer

Z-scores, slowly.

A z-score is a way to rewrite a raw value as “how unusual is this value compared with the training group?” It changes units like years, BMI points, pixel intensities, vessel density, FAZ area, or flow-deficit burden into one common scale.

z = (x − mean) / standard deviation

The plain meaning.

If the z-score is 0, the value is exactly average. If it is +1, it is one standard deviation above average. If it is −1, it is one standard deviation below average.

−2Far below average. Uncommon low value.
−1Below average by one typical spread.
0Exactly the training-set average.
+1Above average by one typical spread.
+2Far above average. Uncommon high value.

Concrete example.

Suppose the training patients have mean age 60 and standard deviation 10. A patient aged 70 has z-score +1. A patient aged 45 has z-score −1.5.

Raw valueCalculationZ-scoreMeaning
Age 60(60 − 60) / 100Average age in the training fold.
Age 70(70 − 60) / 10+1One standard deviation older than average.
Age 45(45 − 60) / 10−1.5One and a half standard deviations younger than average.

Why not just use raw numbers?

Because raw variables live on different scales. Age might range from 30 to 90. BMI might range from 18 to 45. Pixel values might have another range. Neural networks train better when inputs arrive on comparable scales.

Before z-scoring

Large-scale variables can dominate small-scale variables just because their numbers are bigger, not because they are more clinically important.

After z-scoring

Every continuous variable is expressed in “distance from normal for this dataset.” The model sees age, BMI, and intensities in a comparable numeric language.

Negative values are normal

A negative z-score does not mean impossible or bad. It only means below the training-set average. This is why the plan uses GELU instead of ReLU for clinical features, ReLU would erase many negative values.

Critical rule: training stats only.

For each fold, compute the mean and standard deviation on the training patients only. Then reuse those exact numbers for validation and test patients.

Why: if validation or test data help compute the mean or standard deviation, information leaks from evaluation into training. That makes results look better than they really are.
