Phase 18 — Publication

What Did We Invent?

A research paper is only as strong as its novel contributions. Phase 18 identifies and articulates the seven specific methodological innovations that make this work publishable. Each contribution is framed as a clear, falsifiable claim: here is what we did, why it is new, and what evidence supports it.

Reviewer Question

"What is the novel contribution of this work?" If you cannot answer in one sentence per contribution, the paper lacks a clear contribution statement.

Why We Need to Explicitly Define Contributions

Every research paper needs a "Contributions" section that tells reviewers exactly what is new. A common mistake is to describe what the system does ("we built a multimodal classifier for OCTA images") without explaining what is novel about it. Phase 18 pre-writes these contribution statements so they can be directly incorporated into the paper's Introduction and Discussion sections.

For beginners: A "contribution" is something that was not known or not possible before your work. Building a standard ResNet on a new dataset is not a contribution (you just applied an existing method). But inventing a new way to use bilateral eyes for self-supervised learning is a contribution. Phase 18 helps you distinguish between "work" and "contribution."
Contribution 1

Bilateral Natural Positives for OCTA-SimCLR

What it is

A self-supervised contrastive pretraining strategy that uses the two eyes of the same patient (OD and OS) as natural positive pairs. For patients with only one eye, standard augmented views are used as fallback.

Why it is novel

Prior SimCLR applications in medical imaging rely on data augmentation to create positive pairs. This is the first work to exploit the bilateral structure of ophthalmic data as a free source of medically meaningful positive pairs. The two eyes of the same patient share the same systemic disease state, making the positive-pair signal stronger and more specific than augmented views of a single image.

Evidence

Ablation Phase 15: removing SimCLR pretraining reduces macro-AUC by X (measured in experiment). Analysis of positive-pair composition shows that bilateral pairs contribute ~60% of total positive pairs in the dataset.

Contribution 2

Layer-Identity Tokens in Cross-Layer Attention

What it is

Learnable identity embeddings attached to each retinal layer's feature token before multi-head self-attention. The three tokens (Sup, Deep, CC) each have a unique, trainable vector added to their features so the attention mechanism can distinguish which layer each token represents.

Why it is novel

Without identity tokens, self-attention treats the three tokens as an unordered set -- swapping Sup and Deep produces the same attention scores because the attention mechanism has no concept of "token 0 is always SVC." Layer-identity embeddings fix this and, through the learned attention weights, provide an interpretable measure of which retinal layer dominates the classification decision for each cohort.

Evidence

Ablation Phase 15: removing identity embeddings reduces macro-AUC by X. Attention weight analysis (Phase 13) shows distinct layer-dominance patterns across cohorts, confirming the tokens carry layer-specific information.

Contribution 3

Learnable Mask-Token Imputation for Clinical Data

What it is

Instead of filling missing clinical values with the mean or median, each feature has a learnable scalar token that represents "not measured." The model learns during training what value to substitute when a feature is absent.

Why it is novel

Mean/median imputation assumes missing values are "average" -- but missingness in clinical data is often informative. Sick patients may be less likely to have certain tests completed. A learned mask token can represent "this value was not measured, and that fact itself may correlate with disease status." This is a simple but effective idea that is rarely applied in medical imaging pipelines.

Evidence

Compared to mean imputation, learned mask tokens improve performance on samples with missing clinical data (measured in ablation). The learned mask token values, which can be inspected, show systematic offsets from the population mean for several features.

Contribution 4

Prototype-Augmented Classification for Rare Cohorts

What it is

A hybrid classification head that combines a standard linear classifier with a non-parametric prototype-based classifier. During training, class prototypes are maintained as exponential moving averages of class-conditional penultimate-layer embeddings. At inference, the final logit is the sum of the linear logit and the negative Euclidean distance to each class prototype, weighted by a learned scalar.

Why it is novel

Rare cohorts (like MRCC, the smallest in RASTA) have few training examples, making linear classifiers prone to overfitting. Prototype-based classification provides a non-parametric signal that generalizes better for small classes because it directly measures similarity to known examples. The hybrid approach gets the best of both worlds: parametric capacity for well-represented classes and non-parametric robustness for rare ones.

Evidence

Ablation Phase 15: removing the prototype head reduces per-class recall for the smallest cohort (MRCC) more than for the largest (RETINORM). Prototype weights learned during training are higher for rarer classes, confirming the adaptive behavior.

Contribution 5

Layer-Dominance Analysis as a Clinical Explainability Tool

What it is

A systematic method for attributing classification decisions to specific retinal layers by extracting attention weights from the cross-layer attention module and computing per-sample and per-cohort layer-dominance scores.

Why it is novel

Standard explainability methods (Grad-CAM, SHAP) explain where in an image the model is looking. Layer-dominance analysis explains which retinal depth is most important -- a clinically meaningful distinction. An ophthalmologist cares not just about "where in the image" but "which vascular layer." This bridges the gap between deep learning explanations and clinical reasoning.

Evidence

Layer-dominance scores show distinct patterns across cohorts (e.g., MRCC relies more on SVC, FAMILIPO relies more on CC). These patterns are consistent with known pathophysiology and are supported by per-layer Grad-CAM overlays.

Contribution 6

Uncertainty-Aware Disease Classification

What it is

Integration of Monte Carlo Dropout for epistemic uncertainty estimation, post-hoc temperature scaling for calibrated probabilities, and a clinical triage mechanism that flags uncertain predictions for manual review.

Why it is novel

Most medical imaging classifiers produce point predictions without communicating confidence. A screening tool that says "75% probability of CAD" without conveying uncertainty may lead to inappropriate clinical action. Our system explicitly computes prediction entropy and flags uncertain cases (entropy beyond a threshold) for expert review. This is a practical contribution for clinical deployment, where "I don't know" is often the most valuable answer a model can give.

Evidence

Coverage analysis (Phase 14) shows that the top-30% most confident predictions have substantially higher accuracy than the bottom 30%. Calibration curves (ECE) demonstrate that predicted probabilities match empirical frequencies after temperature scaling.

Contribution 7

Automated Weak OCTA Segmentation for Mask-Guided Classification

What it is

A fully automated pipeline that generates vessel, FAZ, and CC flow-deficit pseudo-masks from OCTA images without any manual annotations. Classical image-processing methods (Frangi vesselness, adaptive thresholding) produce initial masks, which are then refined by a frozen U-Net++ segmentation student trained on the pseudo-labels. The refined masks are consumed by a mask encoder in the disease classifier, and quantitative biomarkers extracted from them are appended to the clinical feature vector.

Why it is novel

Prior work either relies on manual segmentation (expensive, not scalable) or uses segmentation only as a preprocessing step without integrating masks into the classification architecture. Our approach is fully automated, produces interpretable intermediate representations (masks + biomarkers), and demonstrates that mask-guided classification outperforms image-only baselines.

Evidence

Ablation Phase 15: removing the mask branch reduces macro-AUC. The classical-improved masks pass QC at ~99.4%, and the U-Net++ student further stabilizes mask quality. Biomarker stability comparisons (student vs. classical) show reduced variance with student refinement.

How to Use These Contributions in Your Paper

Each contribution above is written as a complete paragraph that can be adapted for different sections of the paper:

  • Abstract: Mention the top 3 contributions (1-2 sentences each).
  • Introduction: State the gap in existing work and preview how your contributions address it.
  • Methods: Describe each contribution in detail with technical specifications.
  • Discussion: Revisit each contribution in light of the results. Did the evidence support the claim? Were there limitations?

A useful framing device: for each contribution, state "Prior work did X. We do Y. The improvement is Z." This three-part structure makes the novelty immediately clear to reviewers.

Glossary for Beginners

Novel Contribution
Something new that your work adds to the scientific literature. Not just "we applied method X to dataset Y" (that is a replication, not a contribution), but "we invented method Z that solves a previously unsolved problem."
Self-Supervised Learning
A training method where the model learns useful representations from unlabeled data by solving a pretext task (e.g., "are these two images from the same patient?"), then transfers those representations to the downstream task.
Prototype-Based Classification
A classification method where predictions are based on similarity to stored examples ("prototypes") of each class, rather than learning a decision boundary. The prediction for a new sample is determined by which class prototype it is closest to.
Epistemic Uncertainty
Uncertainty due to lack of knowledge or data -- the "I haven't seen enough examples like this" kind of uncertainty. MC-Dropout estimates epistemic uncertainty by measuring how much the prediction varies across different forward passes with dropout activated.
Pseudo-Mask
A segmentation mask generated by an automated algorithm rather than manually annotated by a human expert. Pseudo-masks are noisier than ground truth but can be produced at scale without costly annotation effort.
